What Are Cookies?

Cookies are small text files that are placed on your computer, smartphone, or other device when you visit a website. They are widely used to make websites work more efficiently and provide information to the website owners.

Cookies are created when your browser loads a particular website. The website sends information to the browser which then creates a text file. Every time the user returns to the same website, the browser retrieves and sends this file to the website's server, enabling the website to "remember" your preferences and actions over time.

How Cookies Work

When you visit a website that uses cookies, the website server requests permission to store a cookie on your device. If you accept, the cookie is stored as a small text file. When you revisit the same website, your browser sends the cookie back to the server, allowing the website to recognize your browser and remember certain information about you.

Cookies typically contain:

  • The name of the server the cookie was sent from
  • The lifetime of the cookie
  • A value - usually a randomly generated unique number

The website server uses this information to remember your preferences, keep you logged in, track your browsing behavior, or personalize content.

Why Websites Use Cookies

Cookies serve several important functions that enhance the user experience:

  • Authentication: Cookies help websites determine if a user is logged in and under which account
  • Session Management: They allow websites to remember users' actions and preferences during a browsing session
  • Personalization: Cookies store user preferences, themes, and other settings
  • Tracking: They monitor and analyze user behavior to improve websites
  • Advertising: Cookies deliver targeted advertisements based on user interests

First-Party vs. Third-Party Cookies

It's important to understand the distinction between first-party and third-party cookies:

First-Party Cookies

First-party cookies are set by the website you are visiting directly. They are generally used to remember your preferences, login information, and other functional aspects of the website. These cookies are typically considered less intrusive from a privacy perspective.

Third-Party Cookies

Third-party cookies are set by domains other than the one you are visiting. They are most commonly used for advertising and tracking purposes across multiple websites. For example, if a website includes content from a third party (like an embedded video or social media button), that third party may set a cookie through your browser.

Other Tracking Technologies

In addition to traditional cookies, we and our partners may use other similar tracking technologies:

Web Beacons

Also known as "pixel tags" or "clear GIFs," web beacons are tiny graphics with a unique identifier that are used to track user activity. Unlike cookies, which are stored on a user's device, web beacons are typically embedded in web pages or emails and are used to monitor user behavior and campaign effectiveness.

Local Storage

HTML5 local storage allows websites to store larger amounts of data on a user's device. Unlike cookies, this data is not automatically transmitted to the server with each request. Local storage is often used to improve website performance and enable advanced features.

Session Storage

Similar to local storage, session storage allows websites to store data that persists only for the duration of the page session. The data is deleted when the browser tab is closed.

IndexedDB

IndexedDB is a low-level API for storing significant amounts of structured data, including files and blobs. It's more powerful than local storage and is used for complex web applications.

Legal Framework for Cookies

The use of cookies is regulated by various privacy laws around the world:

General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

The GDPR, which applies to users in the European Economic Area (EEA), requires websites to obtain explicit consent for non-essential cookies. The regulation classifies cookies that are strictly necessary for the operation of a website as exempt from the consent requirement.

ePrivacy Directive

Also known as the "Cookie Law," this EU directive specifically addresses the use of cookies and requires informed consent before storing or accessing information on a user's device.

California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA)

The CCPA gives California residents the right to opt out of the "sale" of their personal information, which may include certain types of cookies used for targeted advertising.

Other Regional Laws

Various other countries have implemented their own regulations regarding cookies and online tracking, including Brazil's LGPD, Canada's PIPEDA, and others.

At Grand Gardening Pairs, we are committed to complying with all applicable privacy laws and regulations in the jurisdictions where we operate.

Types of Cookies We Use

We use various types of cookies for different purposes. Understanding these categories will help you make informed decisions about which cookies to accept or reject.

Essential cookies (also known as strictly necessary cookies) are required for the basic functionality of our website. They enable core features such as security, network management, and accessibility. Without these cookies, our Services cannot function properly.

Performance Cookies

Performance cookies (also known as analytics cookies) collect information about how visitors use our website, such as which pages are visited most often, how users navigate between pages, and if they encounter error messages. These cookies help us understand how to improve our Services.

Purpose of Performance Cookies

  • Analyze how visitors use our website
  • Measure website performance and loading times
  • Identify technical issues and errors

  • Track the effectiveness of our marketing campaigns
  • Improve website design and functionality based on user behavior

Data Collected by Performance Cookies

  • Referral sources (how you arrived at our website)

  • Device type and operating system
  • Country and language settings
  • Error messages encountered

How We Use Performance Data

The information collected by performance cookies is aggregated and anonymized, meaning it does not identify individual users. We use this data to:

  • Identify popular content and areas for improvement
  • Optimize website structure and navigation
  • Improve page loading times
  • Fix technical issues and bugs
  • Make informed decisions about website development

Functionality Cookies

Functionality cookies allow our website to remember choices you make and provide enhanced, more personal features. These cookies can also be used to remember changes you have made to text size, fonts, and other parts of web pages that you can customize.

Purpose of Functionality Cookies

  • Remember your login details and preferences
  • Store your language and regional settings
  • Remember customizations you've made to the website
  • Provide enhanced features and personalization
  • Remember if you've already participated in a survey
  • Store your accessibility settings

Examples of Functionality Cookies

  • Preference cookies: Remember your language, region, or other preferences
  • Customization cookies: Store information about how you've customized the website layout
  • Authentication persistence cookies: Keep you logged in during your browsing session
  • Multimedia cookies: Remember your volume settings for videos and audio

Functionality cookies enhance your browsing experience by making our Services more personalized and convenient to use. The information these cookies collect may be anonymized, and they cannot track your browsing activity on other websites.

Targeting/Advertising Cookies

Targeting or advertising cookies are used to deliver advertisements more relevant to you and your interests. They are also used to limit the number of times you see an advertisement and help measure the effectiveness of advertising campaigns.

Purpose of Targeting Cookies

  • Deliver targeted advertisements based on your interests
  • Limit the number of times you see the same ad
  • Measure the effectiveness of advertising campaigns
  • Build a profile of your interests
  • Track whether you have clicked on an advertisement
  • Share information with advertisers about your interactions with ads

How Targeting Cookies Work

Targeting cookies work by tracking your browsing behavior across different websites. This information is used to create a profile of your interests, which is then used to show you advertisements that are more likely to be relevant to you.

These cookies are typically set by advertising networks with the website operator's permission. They remember that you have visited a website, and this information is shared with other organizations such as advertisers.

Our Approach to Targeted Advertising

At Grand Gardening Pairs, we believe in responsible advertising practices. We work with advertising partners who comply with industry self-regulation programs and provide opt-out mechanisms. We never use targeting cookies to collect sensitive personal information without your explicit consent.

Advertising Choices: You can opt out of targeted advertising from many major advertising networks through the Digital Advertising Alliance, Network Advertising Initiative, or European Interactive Digital Advertising Alliance. Links to these opt-out tools are provided later in this policy.

Purpose of Social Media Cookies

Cookie Duration

Cookies can be classified based on how long they remain on your device:

Session Cookies

Session cookies are temporary and exist only for the duration of your browsing session. They are deleted automatically when you close your browser. These cookies are typically used for essential functions like maintaining your logged-in status or shopping cart contents.

Persistent Cookies

Persistent cookies remain on your device for a set period specified in the cookie file. They are activated each time you visit the website that created that particular cookie. Persistent cookies are used for functions like remembering your preferences or login details for future visits.

The lifespan of persistent cookies varies significantly - some may expire after a few days, while others may remain for several years unless manually deleted.
